## 3. Switching Costs

**Diana:** So the third power we're going to discuss is Switching Costs. That is the concept where you get a moat when your customers are kind of trapped because it becomes very expensive for them to find another solution. Even if the other solution might be a little bit better, it's just very painful for them to switch — financially or in terms of the operations, time, or effort — because they just have so much invested in the current solution.

Examples that are given in the book are like databases, like Oracle. When you have all of your system of record and all your data in Oracle, it becomes incredibly hard to migrate. Database migration is something that people just don't do. The other example given is Salesforce, because once you have all your customer records in Salesforce, you build all these workflows, the UI — it's just a lot to retrain your sales team to use new software. You need to migrate all the data, and then at that point, for the company to switch to a new CRM is probably going to lose like a whole year of productivity or something, even if the new solution is a little bit better.

I think how AI companies are building moats with this has to do with a version of what Gary mentioned with the forward-deployed engineer. We've given examples of this with Happy Robot or Salient, where they start with specific workflows that are very customized per company, and they work with large enterprises. Part of it is that with the forward-deployed engineer, they may have very long pilot periods which might last six months to a year. But if they succeed, these convert into seven-figure contracts.

The reason these pilots are so long is because they're very much building custom software for the specific operations in these companies. The example for Happy Robot — they got customers like DHL, where they went deep into integrating with a lot of the workflows for how all their logistics operations are done, which is very custom to the DHL operation. **Jared:** Google was already one of the biggest consumer brands on the planet. It was almost certainly the biggest consumer brand on the internet. And yet somebody else came along and built the brand as the consumer AI app, and Google is playing catch-up.

**Harj:** If someone had told me in 2022 that that's how it would play out, I would have been fairly incredulous.

**Gary:** It's also a perfect example of counter-positioning. I mean, Google had a business model that required it to continue to support ads. And so you have the greatest cash cow in the history of man. Why would you disrupt it, even at the cost of setting back human access to knowledge by a few years? Even if that's the core stated goal of Google itself — to organize the world's information.

**Diana:** There's also the untold story of the origin story of ChatGPT — how it came to be — which is really the original moat for startups: speed. It shipped very quickly, in a matter of months, with a very small team of a couple engineers. I mean, it required Sam Altman and YC Research and Greg Brockman to go hire Ilya Sutskever out of DeepMind, because he was there. And all the people — a lot of the people who went on to help create OpenAI — they came from DeepMind. It was already in the right place. It's just that that place didn't nurture exactly the thing that society really needed.

**Gary:** For speed. So there's that moat again — speed, number one.

## 6. Network Economy

**Gary:** Do you want to talk about Network Economy, Diana?

**Diana:** Yeah. In the book, a Network Economy is described as where the value of the product increases as more users or customers get and use the product, and everyone derives more value as an effect of more people using it. Examples that were given in the book are Facebook — where as you use it and your friends use it, it's more fun for me to use Facebook because all my friends are in there. As more users come in, the social network becomes more valuable. And the other example he gives is the Visa network — where the more merchants are using Visa, the more value the consumer gets because you can swipe the Visa card in more places. Then that becomes the moat, because it's harder to amass this large number of users or merchants in order to win. So that becomes very defensible.

In the current era for AI, the shape of network effects is different. It really comes in the shape of data. I think a lot of the data that AI companies get access to becomes the moat — where the more data they get, the custom models they build become better, and the better models become a better product for users. There are lots of examples of these, and besides the big foundation model companies — they probably use some of the data from the users.

**Gary:** ChatGPT almost certainly feeds a lot of that back, because you have a certain reward function for each training run, right? So all the history of every chat from ChatGPT 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 now gets fed into GPT-6 and so on and so forth. It helps create the next model version.

**Diana:** And there's even smaller versions of this. For example, Cursor — they have probably one of the best tab autocomplete. Because with the free version of Cursor, they actually say it when you sign up, that they will use the data and they use that to train it. And the more users they get—

**Gary:** I think it's all the data. I think it's quite literally every mouse click and every keystroke that you emit when you're using Cursor is fed into a model, which is kind of crazy.

**Diana:** Which then — the more developers use Cursor, the better the product gets, and then they compound a lot of the wins with that. And the version where this applies to AI startups is when they go work with enterprises and large companies, they get access to private data. I mentioned earlier Salient or Happy Robot — when the employees of the companies where they become customers, as they use their product, they have a lot of that private data that makes the workflows better.

And the way they improve that — which is the second way of having moats with networks — is really evals. We've talked a lot about evals being the key moat for AI startups. Evals is where you get a lot of the "this workflow worked or didn't work," and then take that back and iterate and improve your context engineering. And that is a flywheel that you can only achieve when you get more and more usage of your product, whether being a consumer product or an AI vertical SaaS agent.

## 7. Scale Economies

**Diana:** So now the last moat in the book is Scale Economies. Jared, do you want to tell us about it?

**Jared:** Scale Economies, or economies of scale. You've invested a lot of money to build something that's really big, and as a result you have economies of scale and you can offer the service cheaper than anybody else. So the classic example would be UPS or FedEx or the Amazon delivery network. They built massive physical infrastructure, and as a result they have a lower cost per unit compared to a smaller competitor.

I think the way this has played out in the AI world — I don't think it's actually played out that much at the application layer. It's really played out at the model layer, right? Training a state-of-the-art LLM is very capital-intensive. Only a few companies can afford to do it. Once you've done it, you can afford to let people do inference on that model very inexpensively.

This is why the DeepSeek announcement was so earth-shattering last year — because it seemed like it might be a lot cheaper than people previously thought to train a frontier LLM, which would greatly diminish the power of this economies-of-scale moat that people thought the AI labs had.

**Gary:** The key thing about DeepSeek was they figured out and made public this new unlock for models, which is how to do RL. They still built on top of one of the large foundation models, so it's still expensive. The RL part is cheaper, but you still need the very expensive big foundation model. So that's one of the things that the media got wrong.

**Jared:** There's a separate question that people talk about, which is: how will the foundation model companies be defensible against each other? And this is certainly one way — it's very hard to be a new entrant into that game now because of economies of scale.

And we were thinking earlier about how this had played out with startups. There's not that many examples, but I think a good one is a company of yours, Harj. Do you want to explain what Exa does?

**Harj:** Yeah, Exa is essentially search for AI agents. It provides an API for anyone building AI applications that wants to search the web.

**Jared:** And the way I think this is playing out for Exa is: in order to provide that service, they need to crawl the web. Not the whole web like Google does, but a big chunk of it. And that's very expensive to do. It requires a large fixed capital investment. But then once you crawl a big chunk of the web, you can reuse that same crawl for many different customers.

**Harj:** I think what's interesting about Exa — the parallel to the model companies — is that they had invested in that before agents had really taken off. They were fairly early to this. I think they were working on this actually even pre-ChatGPT launching. So they made the investment early on, took a bet, same way that the lab companies took a bet on transformers and scaling laws.

**Jared:** Yeah. And there are two companies in just the most recent batch, Channel 3 and Orange Slice, that are both doing Exa-like plays where they crawl a big chunk of the web, have a big static crawl on their own servers, and then have agents that run on top of that crawl. So I think we're going to see more and more of this, especially as web agents work better.

## Conclusion

**Gary:** You need to mainly focus on the first moat that isn't even in the book, which is speed. If you're really breaking your brain about, "Oh, well, are we going to be a cornered resource or not?" — you're just thinking about it the wrong way. You should not start there. You should start with: do I have a specific person who has some sort of pain point, and it's pretty painful? It's not like, "Oh, it'd be nice if I could do this." It's, "Oh, I am not going to get promoted this year. Maybe I will get fired. This is so painful that I don't want to go to work today." That's the type of pain you're looking for.

And if you can write software or build things that actually alleviate that pain — existential pain, like the business is going to go out of business, or "Oh my god, we could totally take over everything next year" — that's the feeling that you want in your customer. If you can find things like that, go find that and go zero-to-one on that first.
